Something we all use but generally don’t talk about much before/during or after fieldwork. Toilets in the field are usually pretty basic, and in Gabon there were three types, depending on where we stayed:

## The Jungle Throne

There was a little path leading to this outhouse on the edge of the village behind the Chief’s house. It had just one wall, which provided some privacy and also nice views of the forest and an occasional family of village chickens, who liked to visit. It had a toilet seat, which may have been to add some comfort but was not all that comfortable: the wood was rotting, often covered with spiders, and unfortunately occasionally quite messy, suggesting I wasn’t the only person reluctant to sit on it.

{% include gallery id="gallery1" caption="The Jungle Throne" %}

## Park rangers’ outhouse

This is my favourite type of field toilet: a hole in the ground. Squatting is the easiest and most hygienic method, so it was a nice change from the awkward village toilet seat. The outhouse had all four walls, which provided even more privacy but was also a great scaffolding for scary spider webs. There were fewer flies and mosquitoes there at night though (not sure if because of the walls or the spiders), so I guess it was a bonus. 

{% include gallery id="gallery2" caption="The outhouse" %}

## Forest floor

Sometimes the only option available was “the wilderness”: go behind a tree and watch out for ants. And elephants. But really, mostly ants.
