Together with Christine, the Ivorian PhD student investigating *Mecistops cataphractus* movements, we've made a preliminary schedule for our upcoming month of fieldwork. Today was our first daytime survey, which meant radiotracking for Christine and equpment set-up in the field for me. I've now been in Côte d'Ivoire for a week, and am getting used to the heat, but an entire day out in the sun is tough! Our boat is small, and as the water level on the river is fairly low, we're not moving fast: roughly every 100m we hit a fallen tree, a rock, or an exciting combination of the two in the form of rapids. Of course there is always the option to get into the water to cool off (sometimes unintentionally), but I would rather not have to handle all my electronic equpment while soaking wet. The trip down river has been a true "Jungle River Adventure", of some white-water rafting with occasional stops to clear the way for the boat, and the trip back up has been a slow and strenous journey.

The great thing about slender-snouted crocodiles is that, unlike Tomistoma in Mesangat, you can sometimes see them during the day! We spotted two basking on the fallen trees, and even caught a glimpse of one of Christine's large radio-tagged adults staring back at us from behind the vegetation. This may not sound impressive to people used to looking at piles of large crocodiles, alligators or caimans all basking together on the shores of large lakes and rivers, but for me a chance to see one of the rarest crocodiles in the world, **wild** and in **daytime** is incredible!

Taï National Park is also home to a (fairly small) population of forest elephants. Having just spent two months in a place with a large population of these animals, and not seeing any of them, I wasn't particularly hopeful for an encounter. But as we turned a corner going up the river in our little boat, we spotted fresh elephant tracks leading from the water. And then we heard the elephants moving throught the forest. They weren't far from us, perhaps 100 meteres away, and while we couldn't actually see them through the thick vegetation, this almost-encounter still felt pretty special. 

After I set up my recorders, and while getting ready to collect my habitat data, the weather decided I've been hot and sweaty long enough, and we got hit with a rain storm. Assessing habitat though a wall of water is a challenge I didn't need, so we put on our rain ponchos and headed back to the lodge. On the way we also discovered that for some reason the tse tse flies are not just attracted to blue and black, but also, unfortunately, to the dark bottle-green of our ponchos. The rain didn't seem to stop them, so the journey back was an unpleasant reminder of the battles I had with my least favourite insect in Gabon. Christine assured me there aren't that many tse tse here as in some other parts of the country, which did not really encourage me to visit those parts anytime soon...
