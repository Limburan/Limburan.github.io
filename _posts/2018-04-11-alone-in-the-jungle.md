Yesterday morning we went from 15 people + staff in camp, to just two. The film crew finished filming (they managed to see the chimps on Monday!) and everyone was heading back to Abidjan, leaving Christine and me alone to get on with our fieldwork. It felt weird suddenly being here almost alone, but a nice quiet morning coffee while looking at the forest and the fact I now get my very own bungalw, with a real bed and a bathroom, have lessened my fears of feeling too sad, lonely and isolated in the field again. Plus, Christine is here, and it's great to have another PhD student to discuss ideas with, share responsibilities and chat with minimal language barrier (her English is much better than my French, and she's keen to practice, which makes my life *a lot* easier).

Christine has been busy the last few days running errands, catching and tagging crocodiles, piloting the boats and filming, so naturally she was exhausted an needed to crash and rest. Today was out first "oficial" work day, but since I haven't actually had a chance to see the crocodiles here yet, we had to start with a nightime exploratory spotlight survey. 

Every fieldwork has its down sides, and while the life and logistics here appear much easier than in Gabon, travelling on the river is a lot more difficult! We have a small inflatable boat with an outboard, but the low water level and the multitude of fallen trees and exposed rocks in the river make our journeys up and down incredibly slow. We had to get out of the boat several times, stop to cut paths through the tree branches, and navigate with one eye on the crocs and the main lights on the logs in front of us. Luckily the crocodiles don't seem as easily spooked as in Mesangat (I have clearly started my crocodile career with the most shy and elusive species available, so now even the slender-snouted crocodiles seem bold to me), and we managed to catch 6 juveniles to get some calls for my playback experiment. We still need to catch a few more, and Christine's ongoing mission is catching adults, but I felt very successful on our first night out!

We also saw a water chevrotain - possibly the cutest anelope in the world: small, fat and spotty. Unfortunately it was before I figured out my camera's night settings, so am not going to win any wildlife photography awards anytime soon.
