Last night, after about 14 hours of travel, I arrived in Abidjan, met with Matt and some of the group we'll be travelling with to Taï National Park, had a lovely Italian dinner, and went to bed. Today, all refreshed, I had my first, and only, full day in Abidjan - the largest city of Côte d'Ivoire.

Abidjan is definitely big and modern, making Libreville and Bristol feel tiny (to be fair, I think Libreville and Bristol are actually approximatley the same size). It's the largest African city I've been to so far (the quick drive through Johannesburg excluded), and its contrasts and pace were a little overwhelming. Not as overwhelming as going into an Orange store to buy a SIM card - it was even more hip and modern than an Apple Store, too cool for a check out counter, or signs showing you where to go. When you come in (after being checked by armed security gurads), a nice person with a tablet asks you what you need, gives you a ticket with your number and asks you to sit on the sofas in their little lounge waiting area. Large screens show the current number and the picture of the sales assistant you need to go see. Luckily my French is good enough to buy a SIM card, even in the most modern SIM card shop in the world, and I was able to re-connect with the world and head over to the zoo.   

Abidjan Zoo is pretty interesting: it's the site of the captive breeding headstarting programme for Project Mecistops, so has relatively large numbers of Western African slender-snouted crocodiles. The crocs have already nested this year, and new eggs are in incubators, waiting to hatch. The babies will be raised in the zoo until they're large enough to be released into the wild. Apart from the crocodiles, the zoo houses many other West African animal species, including chimpanzees, leopards, an elephant and even lions. The animal enclosures are in varying conditions and the atmosphere is much closer to that of some of the Southeast Asian private crocodile farms, than the modern zoos in Europe. The team from Abuquerque Biopark Zoo have been involved in working on improvements for different animal enclosures, with some success, but it's clear a lot of work still lies ahead.

I also visited the crafts market - again a new experience not found in Libreville (their market closed down after the stabbing attack), and again a little ovewhelming. I've been offered so many animal figurines for a "very good price" I could recreate several zoos, but given how many African animal-shaped decorations I already own, I figured I need to look for something a bit more special. I'm here for 6 weeks, so I've got time.

In the evening we headed over to a restaurant on the lagoon with Matt, Amelia and a Dutch documentary film crew, to film some scenes (I may be briefly visible in the background trying to act casual), have an Ivorian dinner and celebrate my birthday. As the sun set we watched thousands of bats leave their roosts in the city and fly out to forage, some flying directly above us, making us worry about the very real possiblity of bat guano landing in our beers, and the less likely possiblity of contracting ebola this way. Luckily, the restaurant provided coasters to cover the drinks and prevent any beer or disease drama.
