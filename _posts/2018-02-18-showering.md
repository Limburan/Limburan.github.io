I should start by saying that Bongo River is not really *infested* with crocodiles – not that many places in the world ever are. It borders a
magnificent Moukalaba Doudou National Park and is home to populations of three African crocodile species: slender-snouted crocodiles (*Mecistops cataphractus*), dwarf crocodiles (*Osteolaemus tetraspis*) and Nile crocodiles (*Crocodylus niloticus*). All three are protected in Gabon, and slender-snouted crocodiles are also one of the rarest croc species in the world, classified as Critically Endangered on the IUCN Red List. Being able to see all three of them, wild, in the course of one night is a fantastic experience, and I consider myself really lucky to have had that opportunity.

Doing fieldwork and camping for several weeks in the field meant that we all had to wash pretty regularly – if only to make sure the team members stayed on friendly terms. And the only water source available was the river – the same river where we’d spot crocodiles every night!

Not all crocodile species are equally dangerous to humans – while all can inflict a painful bite, the majority of fatal attacks come from the two large, widespread species: saltwater crocodiles (*Crocodylus porosus*) and Nile crocodiles. Attacks most often happen (a) in water, when people are swimming or fishing, (b) on the water’s edge, when people are washing, doing laundry or collecting water. In Sri Lanka, I visited a site where a girl had been taken by a saltwater crocodile. Her parents said she went down to the same spot at the river’s edge every morning to wash, and would often see crocodiles basking on the banks on the other side. One morning, the crocodile wasn’t on the other side of the river, but waiting for her at her water spot! 

Crocodiles are smart, can learn patterns of human and animal behaviour, and use that knowledge to ambush their prey. The Nile crocodiles congregating to catch wildebeests during the mass migrations are one spectacular example of such behaviour, but even going to the zoo and looking at a crocodile exhibit before feeding time will show you the animals know their keepers’ routine. Knowing all this, I was a bit apprehensive of coming down to the same spot on the river every morning to wash…

Another thing important to mention here is that there have been no reported crocodile attacks on humans in the Gamba region, and generally very few attacks reported in Gabon. Part of it is due to the fact that Gabon human population is pretty small and the majority of the people live in the capital. While there is the possibility of attacks from remote regions going unreported, crocodiles and people just don’t come in contact that often here, and when they do, the humans are usually the predators. Being a crocodile researcher puts you in a lot more contact with crocodiles than an average person, but also gives you the advantage of often seeing the crocodile before it sees you (or so I like to tell myself). 

Armed with all my specialist knowledge, I decided to follow the local custom and wash in the river like everyone else. This changed a bit depending on location. In the village, we had the luxury of shallow river bay (no chance for an ambush attack) and often several pirogues tied to the bank, which meant I could comfortably sit in the pirogue and scoop the water to shower myself. The one issue was privacy – while people generally would leave me to shower alone, occasionally someone would be arriving or leaving the village and passing by with an awkward (me) or indifferent (them) exchange of *Bonjour!* For obvious reason, washing in the river always involved wearing some clothes.

When we moved to the park rangers’ hut in the forest, things got a bit more wild and complicated. The bank was steep and the river deep, with fast flowing current. We only had our boat docked, which – although I tried it once – wasn’t that comfortable to wash in. I just made a mess and then had to take ages scooping the soapy water out of the boat. The ecoguard showed me a little path through the forest which had a gentle slope heading down to the river, and said that’s the best place to wash. It was great and finally offered some privacy, but a flat bank at the river’s edge in the forest is exactly where we’d spot crocodiles. So with my paranoia alive again (sometimes it’s a wonder I’m a field biologist when I’m so easily convinced all the animals are out there to get me), I spent 10 mornings making dashes to the river’s edge, filling up a 2L container, retreating 3 meters away and standing behind a tree, having a quick look around for elephants, using the water to shower, and repeating the process until I was done. 

Did it look silly? Probably very. 

Were there large crocodiles or elephants around? Unlikely. We had a generator on every other day, so the noise was probably enough to keep them away. 

Did I get attacked? No. So maybe there’s some method in this madness…
