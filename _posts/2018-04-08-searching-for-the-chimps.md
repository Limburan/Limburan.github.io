The long journey yesterday didn't stop me, Sterrin and the Abuquerque group from getting up at 6am this morning to go look for the West African chimpanzees. Taï National Park still has a relatively healthy population of these animals - these are the ones that have been documented to use tools - and a habituated group lives nearby. Our guide warned us that we are not 100% guaranteed to find them, and to maximise our chances we should have arrived at the Chimp Camp in the forest last night, spent the night there, and then joined a guide who will go "pick up" the chimps at 5am from their sleeping site to track them throughout the day. The guides rotate in the forest, each following the chimpanzee group until nightfall to note their sleeping location and returning to camp for the night. Since we've arrived so late last night, a 7 km night-time hike throught the forest wasn't an option, so we decided to take our chances and see if we're lucky enough to just run into the chimpanzees.

We weren't. After the 2 hour hike to the Chimp Camp we were already a bit tired and sweaty, but as it turned out, that trail was just a gentle stroll compared to the forest trails within the chimp range! We were constantly climbing up or down steep hills, trying to keep up with the guide, who would stop only occasionally to listen for the sounds of chimps calling and drumming on large hollow tree roots. We finally found another guide who was tracking the apes, and who told us that the group moved on from their sleeping site before the guides got to them this morning. So all that was left to do was to sit on top of a hill and listen for the drumming. We wandered a bit further hoping for a lucky break, but by mid-day we were getting exhausted and running low on water, so decided to head back to the lodge. Crocodile fieldwork does not come with the same level of fitness as primate fieldwork, and I've come to the sad realisation that my hiking stamina from the baboon tracking days in Namibia is now just a faint memory. I have become a boat potato.

It's rare to walk through a tropical forest and not see anything interesting, so although we missed the chimpanzees, we saw plenty of other primates, including western red colobrus, black-and-white colobrus and diana monkeys. We spotted a few hornbills, plenty of birds that I don't know (I'm now seriously regretting not buying a bird guide), millipedes, centipedes, butterflies, and some spectacular large trees. We even saw a small cocoa tree on the trail - the first one I've ever seen, which is a bit embarassing given my love for chocolate and the fact that we just drove through a country full of cocoa plantations. One of the plantations is just across the river from our lodge!
