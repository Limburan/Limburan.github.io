The species was discovered by French missionaries and described by J. G. Schneider in 1801.

## Names

### Common name (English) 
Siamese crocodile

### Local names
Buaya Kodok (“frog crocodile” in Bahasa Indonesia), Badas Hitam (“black badas” in Bahasa Kutai)

## Are they endangered?

Yes. *C. siamensis* is listed in [CITES, Appendix I](https://www.cites.org/eng/app/appendices.php) and classified as Critically Endangered on IUCN Red List. Very few small populations still exist in the wild and are constantly threatened by illegal poaching and habitat loss. Many *C. siamensis* are kept on crocodile farms in Southeast Asia, where they’re crossbred with other crocodile species, especially saltwater crocodile *Crocodylus porosus*, New Guinea crocodile *Crocodylus novoguineae* and Cuban crocodile *Crocodylus rhombifer* (Trutnau and Sommerlad, 2006). The hybrids threaten to contaminate the genetic line of the few remaining pure *C. siamensis* and thus are a constant threat to the species.

Siamese crocodiles grow up to about 3.5 m, but their hatchlings are rather small, usually 25 cm long.

At first glance *C. siamensis* looks quite similar to *C. porosus*: the snout is about 1.5 as long as wide at its base and adults are dark olive (sometimes brown) with white bellies. However, unlike saltwater crocodiles, Siamese crocs have a row of 4 large scales behind their head across the back of their neck. They also have an irregular number of scales on the underside of their tail, just behind the cloaca.However, hybrids can have a mix of any of these features.

## What do they do?

Mostly this:

![alt text](/assets/images/cs-move-do-new.gif "Siamese crocodile escaping")

Hardly anything is known about the ecology and behaviour of wild *Crocodylus siamensis*. Apart from Mesangat, few populations are still present in other countries. Unlike saltwater crocodiles, *C. siamensis* is only found in freshwater. Their diet, nesting biology and ecology are currently studied in Mesangat (Behler *et al.*, 2018, Staniewicz *et al.*, 2018).  

## Do they eat people?

Siamese crocodiles prefer to avoid humans. There has been only one instance of a suspected *C. siamensis* attack on a man in Mesangat area and [two](http://www.crocodile-attack.info/data/filter-simple) elsewhere in SE Asia. Almost all crocodile attacks in Borneo are attributed to saltwater crocodiles, which are larger, more aggressive and have been known to eat people (see Ritchie and Jong, 2002). The lack of attacks by *C. siamensis* is probably due to their low number and shy behaviour – you are much more likely to be peed on by a monkey and bitten by a tomistoma than eaten by a Siamese crocodile.

Nevertheless, it’s important to remember crocodiles are large ambush predators and given an opportunity, would probably happily eat a human. So it’s best to swim elsewhere.

## Want to know more? References and further reading

### Books

Ritchie, J., Jong, J. 2002. *Man-eating Crocodiles of Borneo.* Natural History Publications (Borneo), Malaysia.

*Focuses on Crocodylus porosus attacks in Malaysian Borneo. Especially nice to read when you're actually in Borneo, on a lake and surrounded by crocodiles.*

Simpson, B.K. .2006. *Siamese Crocodile Survey and Monitoring Handbook: An Introduction for Conservation Workers in Cambodia.* Fauna & Flora International, Cambodia Programme.

*A great handbook on how to do crocodile surveys - focuses on Crocodylus siamensis in Cambodia, but can be applied to other species.*

Trutnau, L., Sommerlad, R. 2006. *Crocodilians. Their Natural History and Captive Husbandry.* Chimara, Frankfurt am Main.

*All you could ever wish to know about crocodiles.*
