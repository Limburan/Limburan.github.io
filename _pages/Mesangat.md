A few years ago the name Mesangat Lake was familiar to only a handful of people outside of the Muara Ancalong district in East Kalimantan. These days, thanks to the interest and work of many people involved in conservation of local wildlife the name rings a bell to more crocodile enthusiasts and possibly even some people not involved in crocodile conservation at all.

Although a few more researchers have been lucky enough to visit the area in recent years, I hope this blog can serve as a suitable virtual tour to give you a feel of what Mesangat Lake is like and why it would be a shame to lose such an amazing place.

## **Where is Mesangat Lake?**

Mesangat is a wetland located in Muara Ancalong District, Kutai Timur Regency in East Kalimantan. The lake is connected to several small river backflows and inflows from the neighbouring Kelinjau and Kedang Kepala rivers. Lying in the lowlands, at the elevation <20 m above sea level, the lake expands into a large freshwater swamp during the rainy season and the water level differences throughout the year range over 4 m. 

## **So is it really a Lake?**

The word “lake” is a general term we use to refer to Mesangat area (Danau Mesangat). However, it doesn’t look much like a lake and it would probably be much more appropriate to call it Mesangat Wetland. Or Mesangat Swamp.

The region – just like most of Borneo – used to be covered in forest but has been heavily logged since the 1970s and some of the forests around Mesangat were burnt during the forest fires in 1982-83 and 1997-98 [(Chokkalingam et al., 2005)](https://www.ecologyandsociety.org/vol10/iss1/art26/). Some flooded forest is still present in Mesangat, but currently much of the water in the wetland, especially in open areas, is covered with floating vegetation, mainly grass mats and two exotics: water hyacinth *Eicchornia crassipes* and water fern *Salvinia cucullata*. It makes moving around quite difficult.

## **What is so special about Mesangat?**

### **_Endangered species_**

Several species frequently seen in Mesangat are listed on the IUCN Red List as Critically Endangered (CE), Endangered (EN) or Vulnerable (VU). Of these the most interesting for us are the two species of crocodiles: [*Crocodylus siamensis*](/siamensis/) (CE) and [*Tomistoma schlegelii*](/tomistoma) (VU). Moreover, having two croc species living in the same lake is in itself rather remarkable. Other notable species we’ve seen there are Malaysian giant turtle [*Orlitia borneensis*](http://www.iucnredlist.org/details/15509/0) (EN), Storm’s stork [*Ciconia stormi*](http://www.iucnredlist.org/details/22697685/0) (EN) and proboscis monkeys [*Nasalis larvatus*](http://www.iucnredlist.org/details/14352/0) (EN).

### **_Exotic species_**

These are species people have recently introduced to the area, either intentionally or by accident. Such introductions can be very dangerous to the local species as they can often lead to extinctions of the locals unprepared to cope with the invader. Apart from the exotic plants that have been introduced to Mesangat, several species of animals have also found their way in. Of these the frequently seen and harvested apple snail *Pomaceae sp*. and toman fish (Asian snakehead) *Channa micropeltes* raise most concern. Both occur in large numbers and can potentially eat a lot of local species, but their impact on the lake ecosystem is not yet known.

### **_People_**

Aside from occasional scientists and postgrad students cruising around the area trying to catch crocodiles, Mesangat is also an important fishing area for the people from Muara Ancalong village. Trans-migrants from other areas have also arrived near the lake in recent years. Some timber is still harvested from Mesangat, as are fish, snakes, turtles and crocodile eggs. Additionally, the fishermen regularly burn off vegetation during the low water level to improve access to many flooded areas. The effect of these activities on Mesangat crocodiles is not yet known.

### **_Oil Palm_**

In 2008 the rights to Mesangat Lake were acquired by a local oil palm company that planned to plant the area. Due to many protests from IUCN Crocodile Specialist Group, cautions from the Indonesian Government and heavy flooding, the company stopped planting after establishing a several hundred ha and pulled out from the area, leaving approximately 7,000 ha available for conservation. However, Mesangat is still under consideration as ‘suitable for agriculture’.
